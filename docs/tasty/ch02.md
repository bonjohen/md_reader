# Chapter 2: What the Captain Keeps

The demon had been dead since before dawn and the cold had kept the meat from turning. She hung it from the gambrel by its ankles. The last of the blood drained into the trough, and the body swayed once and settled. Then she wiped her knife on her thigh and began.

The skin came first. She scored along the inside of both legs, then down the midline from pelvis to sternum, working the blade shallow enough to preserve the fascia. Demon hide was worth more intact. One careless nick through the underlayer and the hide would begin to spoil within a day, the leather gone soft and useless. She had seen that mistake cost a hunter a season's wages. She peeled the skin in sections, pulling with her left hand and cutting with her right. The hide came away wet.

She knew the map now.

The room was rented from a tanner in Cassavon — stone floor, drain in the center, iron hooks in the beams. She had used it for the last four jobs and the tanner had stopped asking questions after the second payment. Vark paid in advance, scrubbed the room down after, and worked between midnight and dawn when the streets were empty. The tanner understood what she did in his back room the way the rest of the trade town understood: by choosing not to.

She separated the liver and laid it in the lined box she kept packed with snow and salt. The liver was the most valuable single organ per weight. A fresh one from a mature demon — properly drained, cleanly removed, no puncture to the bile sac — could cover her costs for a season. She had seen what the final buyers paid for the same liver after it passed through two more hands. The number was obscene. Anger was not useful. She was the hands.

After the liver came the kidneys, then the heart. The heart carried less value in isolation, but certain physicians in the southern cities prescribed it paired with the liver. The pair commanded a better price than the sum of its parts.

The hands she removed at the wrists, wrapped separately, and placed in the second box. Demon hands carried a value she had always found absurd, but the market set the price and the price was the price. Bone she stacked for later. Marrow was worth extracting only when a buyer specified it in advance. Her current buyer had not.

She cut the major muscle groups from the thighs and packed them in salted cloth. The shoulders next, then the long muscles along the spine.

She stepped back and looked at what remained on the gambrel. A carcass, opened and reduced to its useful components. It still had a face. The face had been unremarkable alive. Death had not improved it. The demon had been young, male, and traveling alone through the passes above Cassavon — moving like someone who did not expect to be watched. She had tracked him for a day and a half and taken him at dawn while he slept beside a dead fire. Clean kill, clean product.

The knock came as she was finishing. She wiped her hands, checked the floor for anything visible from the doorway, and unbarred the door.

Koss stood outside with his hands deep in his coat and his breath coming fast. His eyes found the carcass on the gambrel and slid off it.

"You could have brought it to the depot," he said.

"The depot is three streets from the garrison. I don't carry product past soldiers."

"It's a short walk."

"It's a short walk to prison, too." She stood aside to let him in. "Everything is separated. Liver, kidneys, heart, hide, hands, long muscle, shoulder cuts. Bone if you want it."

Koss moved through the room without touching anything. He inspected the lined boxes, lifting each lid and leaning in — never close enough to smell. Vark doubted Koss could tell a good liver from a bad one without help, but he paid reliably, never went back on a price once set, and did not ask where the corpses came from.

"The liver is clean?" he asked.

"As clean as they come."

"Last time the client raised a concern about the taste."

"Last time the client was blending product from three suppliers and calling it a single source. That is a quality problem I cannot solve from my end."

Koss almost nodded. He looked at the boxes again. "I can take the organs and the hide today. The muscle I'll send someone for tomorrow."

"Tomorrow the price goes up."

"Why?"

"Because storage is a service. I don't keep this room for free and I don't keep product overnight for charity."

Koss produced a purse and counted out the agreed sum. Vark watched him count because she always watched. He finished counting, then added three coins above the sum and placed them on the table beside the boxes.

"For the trouble of tomorrow," he said. "And because I have a question."

She took the coins, counted them herself, and put them away. "Ask."

"There's been talk from the eastern ranges. The houses up there are putting word out for specific product. Mountain stock. Deep-range. Not the usual crossroads kills." He paused. "Someone is paying a premium for fresh material from the interior. Not aged, not dried, not preserved. They want it still warm when it arrives."

"Who?"

"I don't have a name yet. I have the price they're offering and I know it doesn't make sense."

"Tell me why."

"It's too high. By a factor of three, maybe four. Nobody pays that much for mountain stock unless they want something specific inside the meat. Something that doesn't survive the usual preservation."

Vark lifted one of the hide sections from the drying rack and folded it. The hide was heavy — denser than human skin, tougher at the edges where the demon's shoulders had thickened in the deep ranges. She folded it with one hand, gripping and turning the stiff leather. Koss looked away.

She set the folded hide on the table. "I'll think about it."

"Think quickly. The word is traveling east and you're not the only capable hunter working this range."

"I'm the best hunter working this range."

Koss did not argue the point. He gathered the boxes and the folded hides and let himself out into the cold. Vark barred the door behind him and stood alone in the stone room with what remained — waste and inventory. She cleaned her tools. The floor she scrubbed until the water ran clean into the drain. The carcass she took down from the gambrel and wrapped in limed canvas.

The tanner would find nothing in the morning but the smell of lye and salt — his own smells. The coins sat heavy and warm in Vark's coat as she walked the dark streets toward her room.

The number Koss had named was wrong. She filed it away. She would ask around. Carefully.

The cold was the ordinary cold of a trade town in late winter — nothing like the cold in the mountains.
